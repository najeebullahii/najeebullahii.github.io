Diabetes Readmission Intelligence

Analysed 10 years of diabetic patient hospital records to identify the primary risk factors driving 30-day readmissions — and turned the findings into operational recommendations for discharge teams.

Why This Matters

The Cost of Hospital Readmissions

Hospital readmissions within 30 days of discharge are one of the most closely watched metrics in healthcare. Preventable readmissions cost the US healthcare system over $26 billion annually, and hospitals face direct financial penalties when readmission rates exceed benchmarks.

This project analyses a decade of diabetic patient records across 130 US hospitals to identify which patient groups carry the highest risk of early readmission — and why. The pipeline runs from raw data ingestion and cleaning in MySQL through to an interactive Tableau dashboard.

Key Findings

Three Risk Factors That Emerged

Age is the strongest predictor. Patients over 70 showed the highest probability of 30-day readmission. This points directly to a gap in discharge planning for elderly patients — a group that frequently lacks the support infrastructure to manage post-discharge care independently.
The primary driver of readmission is not diabetes. Despite the dataset consisting entirely of diabetic patients, the leading diagnosis categories associated with readmission were Circulatory and Respiratory conditions — not diabetes itself. Diabetic patients with comorbid heart or lung conditions require a different, more intensive discharge protocol.
Discharge destination determines outcome. Patients discharged to home without support had measurably higher readmission rates than those transferred to Skilled Nursing Facilities or placed under Home Health Agency care. Sending a high-risk patient home without structured follow-up is itself a risk factor.
Data Cleaning

Cleaning in MySQL

Null Handling: Missing values originally encoded as '?' were standardised to NULL for accurate aggregation across all calculations.
Bias Removal: Records for patients discharged to hospice or deceased were removed. These patients cannot be readmitted and would artificially deflate the readmission rate if included.
ICD-9 Mapping: Over 700 specific diagnosis codes were grouped into 9 clinical categories — Circulatory, Respiratory, Diabetes, Digestive, Injury, Musculoskeletal, Genitourinary, Neoplasms, and Other — making the dashboard interpretable for a non-medical audience.
Data Quality: The Weight column was dropped — over 90% of values were missing, making it unusable for analysis.
Recommendations

Operational Changes Suggested

The analysis translates directly into three actionable interventions for hospital discharge teams.

Senior Check-In Protocol: Implement a mandatory follow-up call within 48 hours for all patients over 70 discharged to home. This single intervention targets the highest-risk cohort.
Comorbidity-Aware Discharge Instructions: Develop specialised documentation for diabetic patients who also present with Circulatory or Respiratory diagnoses. Standard diabetes management instructions are insufficient for this subgroup.
Home Health Agency Expansion: Increase referrals to Home Health Agencies for patients currently classified as Home (Self Care) discharge. The data shows structured home support significantly reduces return visits.
